Distribution OS memo

The wedge is beverage. The business is the operating system for physical-world distribution.

The opportunity is bigger than "AI for beverage outreach." The real product is a system of record and execution layer for getting products into the places people actually buy them: bars, restaurants, liquor stores, and eventually every real-world retail channel.

Core thesis

Distribution looks relationship-driven, but most of the workflow is repetitive, structured, and data-shaped. That is exactly where AI agents can outperform pure human hustle.

Wedge

Promise a result, not a dashboard: "We get you into 20 new venues in 30 days."

Moat

Every contact, stocking decision, conversion, and placement feeds a compounding data and execution advantage.

The sharper insight

The hidden leverage is not in software alone. It is in recognizing that physical distribution is still bottlenecked by humans even though the workflow follows recurring patterns.

Why this works

  • Venue targeting is list-driven and segmentable.
  • Buyer outreach follows repeatable messaging patterns.
  • Placement success creates structured feedback you can learn from.

Why this matters

  • The category feels relationship-heavy.
  • The work is actually pattern-heavy with a thin human layer.
  • That makes it ideal for an AI-plus-ops wedge.

The real product stack

If executed well, this business compounds through three layers that reinforce one another.

01 — The graph

Data moat

  • Venue profiles across bars, restaurants, and liquor stores.
  • Buyer, GM, or beverage director contact data.
  • Current shelf mix, pricing bands, and distributor relationships.
02 — The agent

Execution engine

  • Find targets and identify assortment gaps.
  • Write and send tailored outreach.
  • Follow up, qualify intent, and book tastings or meetings.
03 — The loop

Compounding feedback

  • Track who responds, converts, and reorders.
  • Learn which messaging works by venue type and buyer persona.
  • Turn every campaign into better targeting and higher close rates.

The right entry point

Do not begin with a directory or a perfect dataset. Start with a concrete distribution outcome and build the data flywheel behind the scenes.

What to avoid

  • Big upfront data collection with no immediate customer value.
  • Platform-first thinking before proving placement outcomes.
  • Complex product scope before you know what converts.

What to sell first

  • "We land placements."
  • Charge per intro, meeting, or placement.
  • Use manual assist wherever needed to guarantee results.

Why the wedge works

  • Immediate ROI is easy to understand.
  • Every campaign generates proprietary market data.
  • Operations can later be packaged into software.

Why YERDRINKN is the perfect testbed

Built-in feedback loop

  • You control the brand, messaging, and product story.
  • You can test outreach and positioning daily.
  • You get conversion feedback without waiting on a third party.

Startup advantage

  • Most founders do not have an in-house demand lab.
  • You can use the brand to validate both the workflow and the economics.
  • Once proven, the same playbook can be sold to other brands.

Monetization path

Phase 1

Outcome-based pricing

Pay per meeting, intro, or confirmed placement.

Phase 2

SaaS + territory workflows

Charge brands or distributors a recurring fee for territory management, outreach automation, and placement tracking.

Phase 3

Data + transaction layers

Earn take rates on distribution deals and offer data subscriptions once the market graph becomes meaningfully differentiated.

First two weeks: execution plan

Week 1

Build the initial machine

  1. Pick one or two cities and define the ideal venue profile.
  2. Assemble a lightweight venue list from public and manual sources.
  3. Enrich buyer names, contact methods, and product fit signals.
  4. Draft outreach sequences for email, SMS, and social follow-up.
Week 2

Run campaigns and close manually

  1. Launch AI-assisted outreach to a small batch of qualified venues.
  2. Track replies, objections, tastings, and placement interest.
  3. Handle tastings and negotiations manually wherever needed.
  4. Document which signals and messages correlate with conversion.

The only risk that matters

This only becomes a real company if it drives actual placements. If the product stops at emails, dashboards, or generic "AI outreach," it dies. The wedge has to be distribution outcomes in the real world.

If that works, beverage is only the starting point. The same system can expand into food, CPG, supplements, and any category that needs help getting products into physical retail.

In short: start as an AI rep, become the system of record for physical-world distribution, and let the moat form through execution.
